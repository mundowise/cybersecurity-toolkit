Amerigo Bonasera sat in Courtroom 3 of the New York Criminal Court. He was waiting for justice. He wanted the men who had so cruelly harmed his daughter, and who had also tried to dishonor her, to pay for their sins.

The judge, a man of formidable physical appearance, rolled up the sleeves of his robe as if preparing to physically punish the two young men standing before the bench. His expression was cold and majestic. Yet, Amerigo Bonasera felt there was something false in all this, though he couldn’t quite pinpoint what.

“You acted like complete degenerates,” the judge said sternly.

Yes, yes, thought Amerigo Bonasera. Animals. Animals. The two young men, with neatly trimmed and combed hair and clear, clean faces, were the very picture of contrition. Upon hearing the judge’s words, they humbly bowed their heads.

“You acted like wild beasts,” the judge continued, “and it’s fortunate that you did not sexually assault that poor girl, for that would have cost you a twenty-year sentence.”

The representative of justice paused. His eyes, framed by remarkably bushy eyebrows, glanced furtively at the pale Amerigo Bonasera, then settled on a stack of case-related documents before him. He frowned, as if what he was about to say went against his own views.

“But considering your age, your clean record, the good reputation of your families… and because the law, in its majesty, does not seek vengeance of any kind, I sentence you to three years in prison. The sentence is suspended.”

Thanks to his forty years of more or less direct contact with pain, as the owner of a funeral parlor, Amerigo Bonasera’s face did not betray the disappointment and immense hatred that consumed him. His young and beautiful daughter was still in the hospital, recovering from a broken jaw, and these two beasts were to go free? It had all been a farce! He looked at the happy parents, who were now surrounding their beloved sons, and thought they were utterly content; there was no doubt, their smiles confirmed it.

A black and bitter bile rose in Bonasera’s throat, reaching his lips through tightly clenched teeth. He wiped his mouth with the white handkerchief he carried in his pocket. At that precise moment, the two young men passed by him, smiling and confident, not deigning to glance at him. Bonasera said nothing; he merely pressed the handkerchief to his lips.

The parents of the beasts followed. Both men and women were about his age, but they dressed in a more American style. They glanced at him furtively. Shame was reflected on their faces, though a triumphant gleam shone in their eyes. Then Bonasera lost control.

“I swear you will weep as I have wept!” he shouted bitterly. “I will make you weep as your sons have made me weep!” He had brought the handkerchief to his eyes.

The defense attorneys, with hands on their clients’ arms, signaled to them.

Years ago, Johnny Fontane’s troubles had kept some of America’s brightest stars awake at night.

Finally, as he sipped his umpteenth drink, he heard the door open. He kept drinking until his wife stood before him. She seemed utterly beautiful, with her angelic face, her soulful violet eyes, and her fragile yet perfectly formed body. On the screen, her beauty stood out even more. A hundred million men worldwide were in love with Margot Ashton’s face and paid to see it on the screen.

“Where the hell have you been?” Johnny Fontane asked.

“Out and about…” was her reply.

Clearly, Margot had misjudged her husband’s drunkenness. She saw him knock over the cocktail table and felt his fingers grip her throat. Johnny was furious, but seeing his wife’s magical face up close, with those captivating violet eyes, his anger faded, and he felt helpless again. Then she made the mistake of smiling mockingly. He clenched his fists, and his right arm drew back.

“Not the face, Johnny! I’m shooting a movie!” Margot screamed.

He struck her in the stomach. She fell to the floor, and Johnny pounced on her. He could smell her fragrant breath as she struggled to breathe. He hit his wife on her arms and tanned thighs. He struck her as he had struck the neighborhood boys years ago. It was painful punishment, but it wouldn’t cause lasting disfigurement, tooth loss, or a deformed nose.

Yet, his punches lacked strength. He couldn’t hit her; something held him back. And she openly mocked him. Lying on the floor, with her dress hiked up to her thighs, Margot shouted, laughing:

“Come on, Johnny, keep hitting if it amuses you!”

Johnny Fontane stood up. He hated her, but he was powerless against her magical beauty. With a nimble dancer’s pirouette, Margot stood up. Facing her husband, she began to dance around him, singing, “Johnny doesn’t hurt me, Johnny doesn’t hurt me.”

“Poor man!” she added in a sad voice. “He entertains himself by spanking me, as if I were a child. You’ll always be a romantic, foolish boy; even in lovemaking, you’re childish. You think it should be as soft and languid as the songs you sang.” She shook her head and added, “Poor Johnny. Goodbye, Johnny.”

Then she went to her bedroom, and he heard her lock the door.

Johnny sat on the floor, his face in his hands. Humiliation and despair overwhelmed him. But soon, the toughness that had helped him survive Hollywood’s jungle made him pick up the phone and request a car to take him to the airport. There was one person who could save him. He would return to New York and seek the man with the power and wisdom he needed, the man who genuinely cared for him, the only man he still trusted. His godfather, Corleone.

The baker Nazorine, a stout man as rough as his massive Italian loaves, covered in a layer of flour, glared at his wife, his marriageable daughter Katherine, and his bakery assistant, Enzo. The latter wore a prisoner-of-war uniform with green lettering on the sleeve, and the mere thought that the scene about to unfold might make him late for his periodic check-in at the Island’s governor’s office terrified him. He was one of thousands of Italian Army prisoners allowed to work in America, living in constant fear that his permission might be revoked. Thus, Nazorine’s little comedy was, for him, a very serious matter.

“You’ve dishonored my family!” he said. “Did you want to give my daughter a little gift to celebrate the end of the war? Do you know they’ll send you back to your dusty Sicilian village with a kick in the ass?”

Enzo, a short but sturdy young man, placed his right hand over his heart.

“Boss,” he said, almost crying, “I swear by the Holy Virgin that I have never abused your kindness. I sincerely love your daughter, and with all respect, I ask for her hand. I know I have no right, but if they send me back to Italy, I’ll never be able to return to America. I’ll never be able to marry Katherine.”

“Enough of this madness,” intervened Filomena, Nazorine’s wife. “You know perfectly well what you have to do. Our cousins on Long Island will hide Enzo.”

Katherine was crying. She wasn’t shapely, nor was her face particularly attractive. Moreover, a shadow of a mustache marred her features. She would never find another man as elegant as Enzo, nor one who would love her with such devotion and respect.

“I’ll go live in Italy. If you do anything against Enzo, I’ll leave home,” she shouted suddenly.

Nazorine looked at her thoughtfully. The girl was tough. He had seen her press her hips against Enzo’s thighs when he had to pass behind her to take the bread out of the oven. If he didn’t take the proper measures, the rogue’s hard, hot “loaf” would soon be in Katherine’s “oven,” Nazorine thought lasciviously. Enzo had to stay in America and become a U.S. citizen, the baker resolved. But it was a difficult matter; so difficult that only one man could solve it: Don Corleone, the Godfather.

All these people and many more received invitations to the wedding of Miss Constanzia Corleone, to be held on the last Saturday of August 1945. The bride’s father, Don Vito Corleone, never forgot his old friends and neighbors, even though he now lived in a grand, luxurious house on Long Island. The reception would take place there, and the celebration would last all day. It was undoubtedly a grand event. The war with Japan had just ended, so no one would be anguished about the fate of a son or relative on the battlefield. The timing was perfect.

Thus, throughout the morning of the appointed day, the house filled with friends eager to honor Don Corleone. They all brought small packages wrapped in cream-colored paper, containing cash. No checks or gift items: banknotes and a card with the giver’s name. The amount of money reflected the degree of respect for the Godfather. A well-earned respect.

Don Vito Corleone was a man to whom everyone turned for help, and no one was ever disappointed. He never made vague promises or excused himself by claiming his hands were tied by forces greater than himself. It wasn’t necessary to be his friend, nor did it matter if one lacked the means to repay the favor. There was only one condition: that the supplicant personally proclaim their friendship to him. And then, no matter how poor the petitioner, Don Corleone took on their problems and rested only when they were resolved. His reward? Friendship, the respectful title of “Don,” sometimes the more intimate “Godfather,” and perhaps, merely as a token of gratitude and never for profit, a small gift, like a bottle of homemade wine or a basket of taralles specially made to be savored at Don Corleone’s Christmas table. Thus, it was all about tokens of friendship, a way to acknowledge a debt to him and that Don Vito, at any moment, had the right to request a small service in return.

On the great day of his daughter’s wedding, Don Vito Corleone stood at the front door of his Long Beach home to greet the guests, all known and trusted people. Many owed their success to the Don, and on such a solemn occasion, they felt entitled to call him “Godfather.” Even the service staff that day consisted of his friends. The bartender was an old comrade whose gift was providing all the liquor for the party, along with his expert bartending services. The waiters were friends of Don Corleone’s sons. The food on the garden tables was prepared by the Don’s wife and her friends, while the bride’s friends handled the cheerful garden decorations.

Don Corleone welcomed everyone—rich and poor, powerful and humble—with equal displays of affection. It was his nature. The guests marveled aloud at how well the tuxedo suited him; they said anyone could have mistaken him for the groom.

At the door, standing beside him, were two of his three sons. The eldest, named Santino but called Sonny by everyone except his father, was admired by the younger Italians, though the older ones regarded him with suspicion. Sonny Corleone was tall for a first-generation American from an Italian family. He stood six feet tall, and his abundant wavy hair made him seem even taller. His face resembled that of a giant Cupid; his features were handsome, but his thick, sensual lips and dimpled chin were almost obscene. Strong as a bull, it was said his wife dreaded the marital bed as much as heretics once feared the stake. Rumors had even circulated that, as a young man visiting houses of ill repute, the most seasoned prostitutes demanded double the rate.

During the wedding celebration, some uniformly plump young women gazed at Sonny Corleone with languid eyes. However, that day, they were wasting their time. Despite the presence of his wife and three young children, Sonny Corleone had his eyes set on Lucy Mancini, his sister’s maid of honor. The girl, aware of Sonny’s intentions, sat at one of the garden tables. She wore a formal gown with a tiara of flowers atop her glossy black hair. She had flirted with Sonny during the past week’s wedding rehearsals, and that morning, at the altar, she had brushed his hand. An unmarried young woman could do no more.

Lucy didn’t care that Sonny wasn’t a great man like his father, nor likely to become one. Sonny Corleone was strong, courageous, always generous, and it was widely known that he had a large, noble, and often tender heart. Unfortunately, he lacked his father’s humility, and his quick, fiery temper often led him to misjudgments. Though considered an excellent collaborator in his father’s business, many doubted the Don would name him his heir.

The Don’s second son, Frederico, known as Fred or Fredo, was the son every Italian parent dreams of. Dutiful, loyal, always at his father’s service… At thirty, he still lived with his parents. He was shorter and stockier than his brother but resembled him: the same Cupid-like head, the same wavy hair, the same thick lips. Yet, Fred’s lips were not sensual but granite-like. Though somewhat stubborn, he never argued with his father or caused him distress over women. Despite these virtues, he lacked the personal magnetism and animal strength necessary for leading men. Thus, he was not considered a likely heir to the family business either.

The third son, Michael, was not at his father’s or brothers’ side. He had gone to sit in the most secluded corner of the garden, though even there he couldn’t escape the attention of family friends.

Michael Corleone was the Don’s youngest son and the only one who hadn’t let the great man guide him. He didn’t have his brothers’ Cupid-like face, and his black hair was rather straight. His barely tanned skin would have been the envy of any girl. He possessed a delicate, almost feminine beauty, to the point that the Don had once doubted his youngest son’s masculinity. Fortunately, those concerns vanished when Michael turned seventeen.

Michael had sat at the most secluded garden table, as if to signal his voluntary separation from the family. Beside him was the girl everyone had heard of but no one had seen until then. Michael had behaved well, naturally, introducing her to all the guests and his family. Truth be told, the girl hadn’t made a great impression. She seemed too thin, too refined, and her face too intelligent for a woman. Not to mention her manners, far too uninhibited for an unmarried girl, and her name, which sounded so strange to everyone present. Her name was Kay Adams, and if she had told the other guests that her family had lived in America for over two hundred years and that her name was quite common, they would have shrugged.

Everyone noticed that the Don paid little attention to his third son. Michael had been his favorite before the war and thus the presumed heir to the family business when the time came. He had inherited his father’s quiet strength and intelligence, with an innate manner that earned everyone’s respect. But when World War II broke out, Michael Corleone enlisted in the Marines, openly defying his father’s wishes.

Don Corleone had no desire or intention of letting his youngest son die in the service of a country he considered foreign. Secret arrangements were made, and some doctors were bribed. It cost a lot of money, but Michael was twenty-one, and nothing could be done against his will. In the end, he enlisted. He fought in the Pacific, rose to captain, and received several decorations. In 1944, Life magazine published a photo essay of his numerous exploits.

When a friend showed the magazine to Don Corleone (his family hadn’t dared), after a grunt of disdain, he said, “He performs these feats for strangers.”

Michael was discharged in early 1945 due to an injury, unaware that his father had arranged for his release. He stayed home for a few weeks but then, without consulting anyone, enrolled at Dartmouth College in Hanover, New Hampshire. He hadn’t returned to his father’s home since, except now to attend his sister’s wedding and introduce his family to his future wife, that pale American girl.

Michael Corleone had retreated to that garden corner to share gossip and anecdotes about some of the guests with Kay Adams. He enjoyed seeing that Kay found all these people picturesque, and as always, he loved her interest in everything unfamiliar to her. Finally, Kay’s attention settled on a small group of men gathered around a barrel of homemade wine. The group consisted of Amerigo Bonasera, the baker Nazorine, Anthony Coppola, and Luca Brasi. With her usual sharpness, she remarked that none of the four seemed particularly happy.

“No, they’re not,” Michael replied, laughing. “They’re waiting to see my father in private. They all have favors to ask.”

Indeed, the four men kept their eyes fixed on the Don.

As Don Corleone warmly greeted arriving guests, a black Chevrolet stopped at the driveway entrance. Its two occupants pulled out notebooks and, without any pretense, began noting the license plates of the parked cars.

“They must be cops,” Sonny said, turning to his father.

“The street isn’t mine. Let them do what they want,” Don Corleone replied, shrugging.

Sonny’s rough features flushed with anger.

“Those filthy rats respect nothing,” he shouted.

He descended the house’s steps and headed toward the parked Chevrolet. Furious, he confronted the driver, who, without blinking, simply showed him a green identification card. Sonny stepped back without a word and spat on the car’s trunk. He assumed the driver would get out to demand an explanation, but nothing happened.

“They’re FBI,” he told his father upon returning to the door. “They’re noting our guests’ license plates. The damn pigs!”

Don Corleone knew exactly who they were. He had warned his closest friends not to attend the party in their own cars. Though he disapproved of his eldest son’s behavior, the outburst wasn’t entirely useless; it surely convinced the federal agents that their presence was unexpected. Thus, Don Corleone wasn’t angry. He had learned years ago that one must endure some insults, and he also knew that in this world, there always comes a moment when even the humblest man, if he keeps his eyes open, can take revenge on the most powerful. This was what kept the Don from losing the humility that always characterized him and that his friends so admired.

In the backyard garden, the band began to play. All the guests had arrived. Don Corleone forgot about the intruders and, accompanied by his two eldest sons, headed to the celebration.

The vast garden was filled with hundreds of people. Some danced on the makeshift wooden dance floor adorned with flowers; others sat at long tables laden with delicious food and red wine. The young bride, Connie Corleone, was at a slightly elevated table with the groom, the maids of honor, and some attendants. Everything was arranged in the old Italian style. It wasn’t to Connie’s taste, but she had agreed to please her father, considering she had already displeased him enough by choosing her now-husband.

The groom, Carlo Rizzi, was the son of a Sicilian father and a northern Italian mother, from whom he inherited his blond hair and blue eyes. His parents lived in Nevada, but Carlo had left that state due to a minor run-in with the law. In New York, he met Sonny Corleone and, through him, Connie. Naturally, Don Corleone sent some friends to Nevada to investigate Carlo’s trouble with the police: it was a mere youthful indiscretion with a gun, nothing serious, so it was easily ensured that it went unrecorded, leaving Carlo’s record spotless. Additionally, the Don’s emissaries took the opportunity to gather detailed information about gambling in Nevada, sparking Corleone’s interest in possibly making a significant investment in Las Vegas. Part of the Don’s greatness lay in his ability to make the most of everything.

Connie Corleone was not a beauty. Thin and nervous, she was likely to become a grumpy old woman. But that day, in her white wedding dress with a virginal air, she seemed almost beautiful. Under the wooden table, her hand rested on one of Carlo’s strong thighs, while her thick Cupid’s lips sent a kiss to her now-husband. She found Carlo incredibly handsome.

Still very young, Carlo Rizzi had worked as a laborer in Nevada, and from those years, he retained tremendously muscular arms and shoulders that threatened to burst his tuxedo. He gazed into his wife’s loving eyes and poured her wine. He was ostentatiously polite with her, as if acting in a play. Yet, his eyes frequently drifted to the silk purse on the bride’s right shoulder, already stuffed with envelopes of money. How much was there? Ten thousand? Twenty thousand? Carlo Rizzi smiled. It was just the beginning. After all, he was now part of the family. They would have to support him.

Among the guests, a handsome young man with a ferret-like face also had his eyes fixed on the silk purse. Out of pure habit, Paulie Gatto wondered how he might get hold of the bulging bag. The idea amused him, though he knew it was madness, an innocent dream like children shooting tanks with toy guns. He looked at his boss, Peter Clemenza, a fat, middle-aged man dancing lively tarantellas with young girls. Clemenza, immensely tall and heavy, danced with such mastery and abandon that, despite his prominent belly lasciviously bumping into his young dance partners’ breasts, everyone applauded him. When a dance ended, older women grabbed his arm to be his next partner. The younger men respectfully withdrew from the dance floor and clapped to the mandolin music. Exhausted at last, Clemenza sat down. Paulie Gatto served him a glass of cold red wine and wiped his sweaty forehead with a silk handkerchief. Clemenza panted like a whale. He drained the glass and, instead of thanking Paulie, said gruffly:

“The role of dance contest judge doesn’t suit you. Get back to your job. Take a walk out there and make sure everything’s in order.”

Without comment, Paulie vanished into the crowd just as the musicians took a brief break. Then, a young man named Nino Valenti picked up a mandolin, propped his left foot on a chair, and began singing an obscene Sicilian song. Nino Valenti’s face was finely featured, but it was beginning to show traces of alcohol. He kept his eyes half-closed as his tongue caressed the crude lyrics. The women shrieked with delight, and the men chanted the last word of each verse.

Don Corleone, particularly averse to such displays, and despite his stout wife shouting joyously with the other women, discreetly slipped back into the house. Sonny Corleone hurried to the bride’s table and sat next to Lucy Mancini, the maid of honor. There was no danger. His wife was in the kitchen, putting the final touches on the wedding cake. Sonny whispered a few words in the girl’s ear, and she stood up. A few minutes later, he followed her, stopping occasionally to exchange words with guests to avoid suspicion.

All eyes followed him. The maid of honor, fully Americanized by three years of high school, was a tall girl with a certain “reputation.” During the wedding rehearsals, she had flirted with Sonny Corleone casually, as if it were no big deal, thinking there was nothing wrong with joking a bit with the bride’s brother. At that moment, lifting her dress slightly to avoid brushing the grass, Lucy Mancini headed into the house, smiling with feigned innocence. Once inside, she made her way to the bathroom, where she stayed briefly. When she emerged, Sonny Corleone, waiting on the upstairs landing, motioned for her to come up.

From behind the closed window of Don Corleone’s office, Thomas Hagen watched the garden celebration. Behind him, the walls were lined with shelves full of law books. Hagen was the Don’s lawyer and consigliere, a role of utmost importance in the family, despite not being a member. In that room, he and the Don had solved many problems, some quite thorny. So, when he saw the Godfather leave the party and enter the house, he understood that, despite the wedding, they would have some work to do. The Don was likely coming to see him. Then he saw Sonny whisper in Lucy Mancini’s ear and witnessed the little comedy that followed. He hesitated about informing the Don but decided it was better not to. He returned to his desk, opened a drawer, and pulled out a list of people granted permission to speak privately with the Don. When the Don entered the room, Hagen handed him the list. Don Corleone nodded.

“Leave Bonasera for last,” he instructed.

Hagen went out to the garden, where those who had requested a meeting were gathered around the wine barrel, and pointed to the baker, the stout Nazorine.

Don Corleone greeted the burly man with an embrace. As children, they had played together in Italy, and their friendship had never wavered. Every Easter, Don Corleone received cakes as large as truck wheels, made of cheese and wheat, with golden crusts. At Christmas and family occasions, all sorts of pastries from Nazorine’s oven proclaimed the respect he felt for the Don. For years, good and bad, Nazorine had faithfully paid his tribute to the bakers’ union organized by the Don. He had never asked for a favor, except for sugar ration coupons during the war. Now, the time had come to claim his rights as a loyal friend, and Don Corleone would be delighted to help him.

Don Vito gave the baker a Di Nobili cigar and a glass of golden Strega, placing his hand on Nazorine’s shoulder to encourage him to speak—a clear sign of the Don’s humanity. From bitter experience, he knew how much courage it took to ask a friend for a favor.

The baker told the story of his daughter and Enzo, a fine Italian boy from Sicily, captured by American troops, sent to the United States as a prisoner of war, and released on parole to fill jobs left by those fighting at the front. A great love had blossomed between the honest Enzo and the pure Katherine, but now that the war was over, the poor boy would be repatriated to Italy, and she would surely die of grief. Only Godfather Corleone could help the young lovers. He was their last hope.

The Don and Nazorine paced the room, the Don’s hand always on the baker’s shoulders. Don Corleone understood perfectly—his affirmative gestures showed it—the problem. When the baker finished, Don Corleone smiled warmly.

“Stop worrying, my friend,” he said.

He then carefully explained what needed to be done. He would speak with the district’s congressman, who would ensure Enzo became an American citizen. Surely, Congress would not object, as congressmen often help each other. Don Corleone added that it would cost money, about two thousand dollars, more or less, and that he would personally cover it all. Did his friend Nazorine have any objections?

The baker shook his head vigorously. He would never have dared expect such a favor for nothing. And he knew that a special act of Congress was no small feat.

The baker was nearly in tears with gratitude. Don Corleone escorted him to the door, assuring him that people in charge of the details and paperwork would visit him. Before heading back to the garden, the baker embraced him emotionally.

“Nazorine will make a good deal,” Hagen observed, smiling. “He’ll get a son-in-law and a cheap, lifelong assistant, all for two thousand dollars.” After a brief pause, he added, “Who should I assign this to?”

“Not our paesano,” Don Corleone replied after a moment’s thought. “Give it to the Jew in the next district. Now that the war is over, I expect we’ll have many similar cases. We should have more people in Washington to handle the work ahead without raising prices.”

Hagen jotted in his notebook: “Not Congressman Luteco, but Fischer.”

The second man Hagen brought in was tormented by a simple problem. His name was Anthony Coppola, the son of a man Don Corleone had worked with in his youth laying railroad tracks. He needed five hundred dollars to open a pizzeria and cover the deposit for furniture and equipment, including a special oven, and for reasons not relevant, he was denied credit. The Don pulled a wad of bills from his pocket and counted, but it wasn’t enough.

“Lend me a hundred dollars. I’ll pay you back Monday when I go to the bank,” he said to Tom Hagen, smiling.

Coppola quickly assured him that four hundred would suffice, but Don Corleone gave him a friendly pat on the shoulder.

“This wedding has left me a bit short of cash,” he confessed humbly, as if apologizing.

Don Corleone took the money Hagen handed him, added it to what he had pulled from his pocket, and gave it all to Anthony Coppola.

Hagen couldn’t hide his admiration. The Don always insisted that a truly generous man does favors personally. Surely, Anthony Coppola felt honored seeing a man like the Don borrow money for him. Of course, Coppola knew the Don was a millionaire, but how many millionaires would do for a poor friend what Corleone had just done?

After Coppola left, the Don looked at Hagen questioningly.

“He’s not on the list, but Luca Brasi wishes to see you,” Hagen announced. “He understands it can’t be in public, but he wants to congratulate you personally.”

For the first time, the Don seemed displeased.

“Is it necessary?” he asked.

“You know him better than I do,” Hagen replied. “He’s thrilled to have been invited to the wedding. I don’t think he expected it. I suppose he wants to thank you.”

Don Corleone nodded and gestured that Luca Brasi could be brought to him.

In the garden, Kay Adams was struck by the violent purple fury on Luca Brasi’s face. Michael had brought Kay to the party so she could gradually understand what kind of man his father was. Yet, Kay seemed to see the Don only as a somewhat unscrupulous businessman. Michael decided to tell her part of the truth, indirectly. He explained that Luca Brasi was one of the most feared men in the East Coast underworld. It was said his greatest talent was carrying out assigned murders alone. Without accomplices, it was nearly impossible for the law to catch him.

“I don’t know how true all this is. What I do know is that he’s some sort of friend to my father,” Michael said, smiling faintly.

For the first time, Kay began to understand.

“Are you saying a man like that works for your father?” she asked, uncertain.

To hell with it, Michael thought. Kay could and should know.

“Almost fifteen years ago, some people tried to take over my father’s olive oil import business. They tried to kill him and nearly succeeded. Luca Brasi took care of them. The result: he killed six men in two weeks, ending the famous olive oil war,” Michael explained, smiling at the end as if he’d told a joke.

“You mean your father was shot by a gang of mobsters?” Kay asked, her voice trembling.

“That was fifteen years ago. Since then, everything’s been smooth sailing,” he replied, fearing he’d gone too far.

“You’re just trying to scare me,” Kay said. “You don’t want me to marry you,” she teased, giving him a friendly nudge in the ribs. “You think you’re pretty clever, huh?”

“I just want you to think it over,” Michael replied, returning her smile.

“Did he really kill six men?” Kay asked.

“That’s what the newspapers said,” Mike replied. “No one could prove it. But there’s another story about Luca Brasi that no one talks about. It must be so terrible that even my father never mentions it. Tom Hagen knows it, but he’s never wanted to tell me. Once, joking, I asked him, ‘When will I be old enough to hear that story about Luca?’ He told me, ‘When you’re a hundred.’”

Truly, Luca Brasi was a man capable of frightening the devil himself. Short and stocky, his mere presence brought unease to any setting. His brown eyes were cold as ice. His mouth, more than cruel, seemed lifeless; thin, rubbery, and purple.

He was reputed to be terribly violent, and his legendary devotion to Don Corleone was well-known. In fact, he was one of the pillars of the Don’s power. There weren’t many like him. He feared neither the police, nor society, nor God, nor hell; he feared and loved no one. But he had chosen, he had chosen to fear and love Don Corleone.

Once in the Don’s presence, the fearsome Brasi became a tame lamb. He congratulated Don Corleone and expressed his hope that the first child would be a boy. Then he handed the Don a package full of cash as a gift for the newlyweds. He had achieved his goal.

Hagen keenly noticed the change in Don Corleone, who received Brasi as a king would greet a subject who had rendered great service—keeping his distance but with respect and consideration. Every gesture, every word from Don Corleone clearly showed Luca Brasi that he was highly valued. The Don showed no surprise, not for a moment, that the gift was delivered personally. He understood.

The amount in the envelope almost certainly exceeded that of the others. Brasi had spent many hours deciding the appropriate sum, considering what others might give. He wanted to be the most generous, to show the extent of his respect, which was why he had wanted to deliver his envelope to the Don in person—a clumsiness the Don graciously overlooked. Hagen saw Luca Brasi’s usually sinister expression shift to one almost cheerful and kind. Before leaving the room, the man kissed the Don’s hand while Hagen, cautiously, offered a friendly smile, which Brasi acknowledged with a polite grimace of his thin, purple lips.

When the door closed behind Luca Brasi, Don Corleone let out a sigh of relief. He was the only man in the world who could make him nervous; a force of nature, uncontrollable by anyone. Dealing with him required the same care as handling dynamite. The Don shrugged. Dynamite could be detonated safely, if necessary. He looked at Hagen questioningly.

“Is Bonasera the only one left?” he asked.

Hagen nodded. Don Corleone seemed to ponder for a moment.

“Before bringing him in, tell Santino to come,” he finally instructed. “I need to teach him some things.”

In the garden, Hagen anxiously searched for Sonny Corleone. He told Bonasera to be patient and headed toward where Michael Corleone and Kay Adams were.

“Have you seen Sonny around?” he asked.

Michael shook his head. Damn, Hagen thought, if Sonny spent the whole party fooling around with the maid of honor, there’d be big trouble. His wife, the girl’s family… a disaster. Worried, he hurried toward where Sonny had disappeared half an hour earlier.

Seeing Hagen head toward the house, Kay Adams asked Michael Corleone:

“Who is he? You said he’s your brother, but his last name is different, and he doesn’t look Italian.”

“Tom has lived with us since he was twelve,” Michael replied. “His parents died, and he was wandering the streets with an eye infection. Sonny brought him home one night, and he stayed. He had nowhere else to go. He lived with us until he got married.”

Kay Adams was amazed.

“How romantic! Your father must be a kind-hearted man. Not everyone takes in children when they already have so many of their own.”

Michael didn’t think it worth explaining that Italian immigrants considered four children a small family.

“Tom wasn’t adopted. He just lived with us,” he said simply.

“Oh. Why didn’t you adopt him?” she asked curiously.

Michael laughed.

“Because my father said we had no right to change Tom’s last name. He always thought it would be disrespectful to his parents.”

They saw Hagen and Sonny heading to the Don’s office, and Kay pointed to Amerigo Bonasera.

“Why are they bothering your father with business on a day like this?” she asked.

Michael laughed again.

“Because they know a Sicilian can’t refuse anything on his daughter’s wedding day,” he replied. “And no Sicilian would miss such an opportunity.”

Lucy Mancini lifted her skirt slightly and climbed the stairs. Sonny Corleone’s bloated Cupid face, made even more obscene by alcohol, frightened her, but her week-long game with him had been played with the sole purpose of ending up in bed. Her two college flings had left her feeling nothing and lasted only a week.

During the summer, while preparing for her best friend Connie Corleone’s wedding, Lucy heard the rumors about Sonny. One Sunday afternoon in the Corleone kitchen, Sandra, Sonny’s wife, spoke plainly. Sandra was a rough, kind woman born in Italy but brought to America as a young child. She was strongly built with ample breasts. In five years of marriage, she had given birth three times. Sandra and the other women teased Connie about the torments of the marital bed.

“My God!” Sandra had exclaimed. “When I slept with Sonny for the first time, I nearly died of fright. After the first year, my parts were like macaroni boiled for an hour. When I found out he was doing the same to other girls, I went to church and lit a candle.”

They all laughed. But Lucy felt a tingle between her legs.

At that moment, climbing to meet Sonny, she felt her body quiver with lust. On the landing, Sonny took her hand and led her to an empty room. When the door closed behind them, Lucy realized her legs were trembling. She felt Sonny’s mouth on hers; his lips tasted of tobacco and alcohol…

They lay in bed, side by side, close together, catching their breath.

They heard knocks at the door. Perhaps they had been knocking for a while, but they hadn’t noticed. Sonny quickly put on his pants and blocked the door with his foot to keep it closed. Lucy hurriedly adjusted her dress, her eyes blazing. Anyway, she thought, no one would notice. Then they heard Tom Hagen’s low voice.

“Sonny, are you there?”

“Yes, Tom. What’s up?” Sonny said, sighing with relief.

“The Don wants you in his office, now,” Hagen explained, still in a low voice.

They heard him walk away. Sonny waited a moment, gave Lucy a hard kiss on the lips, and then headed to his father’s office.

Lucy combed her hair. She finished adjusting her dress and fixed her garters. Her body was bruised, and her lips felt more sensitive and fuller than ever. She left the room and went straight to the garden. She sat at the bridal table next to Connie, who exclaimed petulantly:

“Lucy, where were you? You look like you’ve been drinking. Stay by my side.”

The bride filled Lucy’s glass with wine and smiled slyly. Lucy didn’t care; trembling, she raised the glass to her mouth and drank, her eyes eagerly searching for Sonny Corleone through the glass. No one else mattered to her.

“Just a few more hours, and you’ll know what’s good,” she whispered mischievously in Connie’s ear.

The bride let out a polite giggle while Lucy, with feigned modesty, clasped her hands on the table. She felt wickedly triumphant, as if she had stolen a valuable treasure from the bride.

Amerigo Bonasera followed Hagen to the office, where he found Don Corleone seated behind an imposing desk. Sonny Corleone stood by the window, looking out at the garden. For the first time that afternoon, the Don was cold. He neither embraced nor shook hands with the visitor. The pale undertaker owed his invitation to the fact that his wife and the Don’s were close friends. As for Amerigo Bonasera, the Don was deeply resentful of him.

Bonasera began his request skillfully, with much circumlocution.

“You must excuse my daughter, your wife’s goddaughter, for not coming today. She’s still in the hospital.”

He glanced at Sonny Corleone and Tom Hagen, indicating he didn’t want to speak in front of them. But the Don refused to take the hint.

“We all know the misfortune your daughter has suffered,” Don Corleone said. “If I can help her in any way, you only need to ask. After all, my wife is her godmother. I’ve never forgotten that honor.”

This was a rebuke. The undertaker had never called Don Corleone “Godfather.”

“Can I speak with you alone?” Bonasera asked, blushing.

“I have complete trust in these two men,” Don Corleone said, shaking his head. “They are my right hand. I cannot insult them by sending them out of this room.”

Bonasera closed his eyes for a moment and then began to speak. His voice was barely audible, the same tone he used to console the bereaved.

“I gave my daughter an American education. I believe in America. This country has made my fortune. I gave the girl complete freedom, but I always taught her never to do anything that would shame her family. She befriended a non-Italian boy. She went to the movies with him, came home late… But the boy never came to greet us, her parents. I accepted it all without protest; the fault was mine. Two months ago, he and another boy took her for a drive. They made my daughter drink whiskey and then tried to abuse her. She resisted, she preserved her honor. Then they beat her like an animal. When I went to the hospital, her eyes were black, her nose broken, her jaw shattered. The poor girl wouldn’t stop crying. ‘Why did they do this, Papa? Why did they have to do this to me?’ I couldn’t help myself; I cried too.”

Bonasera could say no more. He was sobbing, though his voice had not betrayed the emotion he felt.

Don Corleone, as if against his will, made a gesture of sympathy, and Bonasera continued, his voice now broken with pain:

“Why did I cry in the hospital? She was the light of my life, a loving and beautiful daughter. She trusted people, but now she’ll never trust anyone again. She’ll never be beautiful again.”

He was trembling, and his usually pale face had taken on a deep crimson hue.

“I went to the police,” he continued, “like any good American, and the two boys were arrested. The evidence was overwhelming. They pleaded guilty, and the judge sentenced them to three years in prison, but he suspended the sentence. They were released the same day. I stood in the courtroom, realizing I had made a fool of myself. As they passed, those two smirked at me. At that moment, I told my wife: ‘We must go to Don Corleone if we want justice.’”

The Don bowed his head in respect for Bonasera’s pain. But when he spoke, his words were cold, laced with the chill of offended dignity.

“Why did you go to the police? Why didn’t you come to me from the start?”

“What do you want from me?” Amerigo Bonasera said in a barely audible voice. “Ask me for anything, but grant my plea.”

Despite his words, his tone carried a hint of insolence.

“And what is it you ask of me?” Don Corleone said in a grave voice.

Bonasera glanced at Hagen and Sonny Corleone and shook his head. The Don, still seated at Hagen’s desk, leaned toward the undertaker. Bonasera hesitated. Then he brought his lips to the Don’s hairy ear, almost touching it. Don Corleone listened as a priest would in the confessional: with an absent gaze, impassive, remote. They remained like that for a long time. Finally, Bonasera straightened, stepped back from the Don, who looked at him gravely, and with a flushed face, held his gaze.

“That, I cannot do,” the Don finally said. “There’s nothing to be done.”

“I’ll pay anything you ask,” Bonasera said loudly and clearly.

At these words, Hagen shook his head nervously. Sonny Corleone, arms crossed, smiled sardonically and stepped away from the window to approach the other three.

Don Corleone rose, his face as impassive as ever.

“You and I have known each other for many years,” he said in a voice as cold as death. “Yet, until today, you never asked for my advice or help. I can’t even recall the last time you invited me to your house for coffee, though my wife is your only daughter’s godmother. Let’s be frank: you rejected my friendship because you didn’t want to owe me anything.”

“I didn’t want to get into trouble,” Bonasera murmured.

The Don raised his hand in disagreement.

“No. Don’t speak. You thought America was a paradise. You had a good business, lived well. You thought the world was an Eden from which you could take all the good things. You never bothered to surround yourself with true, loyal friends. After all, you had the police and the courts to protect you. Nothing bad could happen to you or yours. You had no need for Don Corleone. Fine. You’ve hurt my feelings, and I’m not one to give my friendship to those who don’t value it, who don’t hold me in regard.”

The Don paused briefly and, before continuing, gave Bonasera a polite yet ironic smile.

“Now you come to me saying, ‘Don Corleone, I want you to do justice.’ And you don’t know how to ask with respect. You don’t offer me your friendship. You come to my house on my daughter’s wedding day, ask me to kill someone, and say”—here the Don mimicked Bonasera’s voice and gestures—“‘I’ll pay anything you ask.’ No, no. I bear you no grudge, but can you tell me what I’ve done to you to be treated with such complete lack of respect?”

“America has been good to me. I wanted to be a good citizen and for my daughter to be American,” Bonasera said, his voice choked with anguish and fear.

The Don clapped his hands.

“Well said, very well said. Then you have nothing to complain about. The judge has passed sentence. America has passed sentence. When you visit your daughter in the hospital, bring her a bouquet of flowers and a box of chocolates; that will comfort her. Cheer up, man! After all, it wasn’t serious; the boys were young and lively, and one of them is the son of a very influential politician. No, my dear Amerigo, you’ve always been honest. Though you spurned my friendship, I must admit that, to me, Amerigo Bonasera’s word is worth more than any other man’s. So, give me your word that you’ll forget all this, as Americans do. Forgive and forget. Life is full of misfortunes.”

The cruel, mocking irony of these words, the Don’s restrained anger, made the poor undertaker tremble, yet he still found the strength to say arrogantly:

“I only ask you to do justice.”

“The court has already done justice,” Don Corleone said dryly.

“No,” Bonasera replied with a stubborn gesture. “It did justice to the boys, not to me.”

With a slight nod, the Don acknowledged the subtle distinction.

“What is your justice?” he asked next.

“An eye for an eye,” Bonasera replied.

“You’ve asked for more. Your daughter is alive,” the Don pointed out.

“Let them suffer as she suffers,” Bonasera conceded.

The Don waited for him to continue. Bonasera gathered his courage.

“How much do you want?” he said desperately.

Don Corleone turned his back, signaling the meeting was over. But Bonasera didn’t move.

Finally, like a good-hearted man who cannot stay angry at a wayward friend, Don Corleone turned back to the undertaker, who was as pale as one of his corpses. There was no doubt; Don Corleone was kind and patient.

“First of all, why do you fear showing me loyalty?” he said. “You go to the courts and wait months. You spend money on lawyers who know full well you’ll only make a fool of yourself. You accept the ruling of a judge who sells himself like the worst of whores. Years ago, when you needed money, you went to banks, paid ruinous interest, and waited, hat in hand, like a beggar, while they pried into your affairs to ensure you could repay.”

After a brief pause, the Don’s voice hardened.

“But if you had come to me, my purse would have been yours. If you had come to me for justice, those pigs who harmed your daughter would have been weeping bitterly long ago. If, by misfortune, an honest man like you made an enemy, that enemy would have become my enemy.” The Don pointed at Bonasera. “And believe me, they would have feared you.”

Bonasera bowed his head.

“I want your friendship. I accept it,” he murmured.

Don Corleone placed his hand on Bonasera’s shoulder.

“Good, you shall have justice,” he assured. “Someday, a day that may never come, I will call on you for a small service. Until then, consider this justice a gift from my wife, your daughter’s godmother.”

When the door closed behind the grateful undertaker, Don Corleone turned to Hagen.

“Give this job to Clemenza and tell him to make sure to use reliable people, people who won’t get drunk on the smell of blood,” he ordered. “After all, though this undertaker’s assistant may wish otherwise, we are not murderers.”

He noticed his eldest son by the window, watching the garden celebration. Don Corleone thought he was a lost cause. If he refused to learn, Santino could never take over the family business, could never become a Don. He had to find someone else, and soon. After all, he, Don Corleone, was not immortal.

In the garden, a loud, joyful shout arose, so loud that the three men started. Sonny Corleone approached the window. What he saw made him rush to the door with a pleased smile.

“It’s Johnny, he’s come to the wedding. Didn’t I tell you?”

Hagen approached the window.

“He’s your godson, after all,” he said to Don Corleone. “Shall I bring him in?”

“No,” the Don replied. “Let everyone greet him. When he’s done, he can come see me. See?” he said to Hagen. “He’s a good godson.”

For a moment, Hagen felt jealous.

“He hasn’t been here in two years,” he replied dryly. “He probably has some problem and wants your help.”

“And who else would he turn to but his godfather?” Don Corleone asked.

The first person to see Johnny Fontane enter the garden was Connie Corleone. Forgetting her bridal dignity, she shouted, “Johnnyyyy!” and threw herself into his arms. Johnny hugged her, kissed her on the mouth, and held her close as others came to greet him. They were all old friends, people who had grown up in the West Side. Moments later, Connie introduced him to her husband. Johnny, amused, noticed the blond, young husband seemed a bit annoyed at losing the spotlight and shook his hand warmly. They toasted with a glass of fine wine.

“Why don’t you sing us a song, Johnny?” someone called from the musicians’ platform.

Then he saw Nino Valenti smiling at him warmly. Johnny Fontane leapt onto the platform and hugged Nino. They had been inseparable, singing and going out with girls together, until Johnny began to gain fame and sing on the radio. When he went to Hollywood to star in films, he called Nino and promised to get him a nightclub contract. But he forgot to follow through. Now, seeing Nino with his cheerful, drunken smile, the old affection rekindled.

Nino began strumming the mandolin. Johnny Fontane placed his hand on his friend’s shoulder.

“This one’s for the bride,” he said, and keeping time with his foot, he sang an obscene Sicilian love song.

As Johnny sang, Nino moved his body expressively. The bride smiled proudly, and all the guests voiced their approval loudly. Halfway through the song, everyone was keeping time with their feet, and at the end of each verse, they chanted the last words, all with double meanings. When they finished, the applause was so loud that Johnny, after clearing his throat, prepared to sing another song.

Everyone was proud of him. He was one of them and had become a famous singer, a movie star who slept with the world’s most desirable women. Yet, he had traveled nearly five thousand kilometers to attend the wedding, showing the respect he felt for his godfather. He still loved old friends like Nino Valenti. Many guests had seen Johnny and Nino sing together when they were just boys, when no one imagined Johnny Fontane would capture the hearts of fifty million women.

After the second song, Johnny jumped down and brought the bride onto the platform, standing between him and Nino. The two men glared at each other fiercely, as if about to fight, and Nino began strumming the mandolin strings furiously. It was an old custom, a mock battle where one sang a verse to provoke his rival, and then the other sang a more biting, mocking verse. In the end, they sang together in harmony. With exquisite courtesy, Johnny let Nino’s voice overpower his and let the bride go with him; in short, he let himself be defeated. When the three embraced at the end, the applause was thunderous. The guests insisted on another song.

Only Don Corleone, standing in a corner, seemed out of place. In a cheerful voice, careful not to offend his guests, he shouted:

“My godson has traveled five thousand kilometers to honor us all; isn’t anyone going to offer him a glass of wine?”
